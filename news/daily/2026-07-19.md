### 1) OpenAI publishes an "AI Scorecard" — useful intelligence per dollar, not cost per token
- Track: ai_change
- Why it matters to me: Sarah Friar (OpenAI CFO) introduces a framework that shifts AI ROI measurement away from token pricing toward **useful work completed per dollar**. The formula — `Cost per Successful Task = Total Full Cost / Number of Tasks Meeting Quality Bar` — includes API costs, human review time, retries, and rework. This directly challenges the assumption that cheaper models are economically superior, which has shaped how I've been evaluating AI tooling.
- Tradeoff / tension: Cheap tokens with low success rates cost more than expensive tokens with high dependability. But measuring "useful work" requires defining what "done" means for each workflow — which is itself a judgment call that varies by domain. Tiered routing (Luna for simple tasks, Sol for complex ones) adds operational complexity.
- Implication: The AI procurement conversation is moving from "which model is cheapest?" to "which model gets the job done reliably on the first try?" This favors models with higher upfront capability even at premium prices, assuming retry and review costs are factored in. For your own projects, stop benchmarking on raw speed or token cost alone — start tracking false-positive rates and human correction overhead.
- Action: Think — audit one current AI workflow and calculate its true cost-per-successful-task including human review and retries. Compare against your current model choice.

- Tradeoff / tension: Deprecating older models saves compute costs and simplifies the product, but each removal signals to customers that their current setup may become unstable. - Implication: Model deprecation is becoming a competitive liability. Companies that retain model continuity (or at least communicate clear migration paths) may win trust even if their latest model isn't the absolute best.
