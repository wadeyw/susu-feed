### 4) Why AI hasn't replaced software engineers, and won't

- Track: ai_change
- Why it matters to me: Directly relevant to AQ1. Arvind Narayanan and Sayash Kapoor, two researchers with academic credibility, take on the AI job-disruption narrative head-on using software engineering as the test case — the profession most susceptible to AI disruption. Their conclusion is that there is enough evidence to reject the mass-layoffs narrative. This isn't a pundit opinion; it's an evidence-based argument from researchers who could easily have chosen the easier narrative.
- Tradeoff / tension: The tradeoff here is between capability and adoption. AI may be technically capable of replacing certain software tasks, but regulatory barriers, accountability requirements, and the coordination cost of replacing human judgment create friction that limits displacement. The implication is that many professions will see partial augmentation (not replacement) because the non-technical barriers to full automation are higher than most people assume. The counter-tension: this doesn't mean no disruption — it means disruption will be uneven, concentrated in specific tasks rather than entire roles.
- Implication: The "AI replaces X" narrative is consistently wrong because it ignores the coordination, accountability, and regulatory layers that slow adoption. Businesses that plan for replacement will be surprised by the slower pace. Businesses that plan for augmentation and task-level restructuring will be better positioned.
- Action: Reframe AI planning from "replacement timelines" to "task restructuring pathways." Where can AI do a good job on specific tasks? Where must humans stay in the loop for accountability? Plan for both.
- Confidence: medium-high
- Source: Simon Willison — https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/14/why-ai-hasnt-replaced-software-engineers/#atom-everything
