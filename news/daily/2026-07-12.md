The benchmark noise (contested evaluations, broken tasks) means real-world testing matters more than published scores.

### 2) AR glasses require continuous recording — and that may be a dealbreaker
- Track: better_decisions
- Why it matters to me: Nilay Patel articulated the fundamental constraint nobody wants to say out loud: making functional AR glasses requires a camera next to your eyes, continuously recording everything, sending data to the cloud. There's no chip small enough and power-efficient enough to do this locally. The conclusion: "if you want to build the product that everyone thinks is the next thing, you are going to have to invade people's privacy. And maybe you shouldn't."
- Tradeoff / tension: Capability requires surveillance infrastructure. You can build a smaller form factor (glasses) only by accepting pervasive recording, or you can respect privacy and accept Vision-Pro-scale bulk with a battery pack somewhere. This isn't a temporary engineering limitation — it's an architectural truth about how computer vision + spatial awareness works. The question is whether society will tolerate the privacy tradeoff or force a compromise on form factor.
- Implication: Any product category that depends on ambient sensing (AR glasses, always-on voice assistants in public spaces, health monitors that record continuously) faces the same structural tension. The companies that solve this will either crack on-device processing (a materials science problem) or navigate regulatory frameworks that define what ambient recording is acceptable. For building businesses: identify where your product faces this same "capability vs. privacy" constraint and plan accordingly.
- Action: When evaluating any product area that requires ambient data collection, ask: "What happens if the answer is 'no, we won't record continuously'?" That constraint often reveals the real product shape.
- Confidence: medium
- Source: Simon Willison quoting Nilay Patel — https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jul/10/nilay-patel/

### 3) Coding agents can write code but fail at the messy adjacent work
- Track: own_business
- Why it matters to me: Stripe's data shows that AI agents are now fully capable of independently writing code and integrating with APIs like Stripe's. But the steps *adjacent* to writing code — deployment, testing, documentation, monitoring setup, error handling — are still too hard for agents to do on their own. Stripe is responding by expanding Stripe Projects to solve this gap.
- Tradeoff / tension: Agents compress the coding step dramatically but don't compress the workflow around it. This creates a new kind of bottleneck: you can generate code fast, but the integration, validation, and operationalization steps become the rate-limiting factor. The business opportunity is in the plumbing, not the code generation.
- Implication: If you're building software products, the agent-resistant parts of your workflow are where competitive advantage lives. The companies that win won't be the ones with the best code generator — they'll be the ones that automate the deployment, testing, and operationalization pipeline. Stripe is betting heavily on this. The same pattern applies to any service business: the value shifts from the core skill (coding) to the supporting infrastructure (making it work in production).
- Action: Audit your own workflow. Which steps adjacent to your core output are still manual, error-prone, or agent-resistant? Those are your highest-leverage automation targets — and potentially product opportunities.
- Confidence: high
- Source: Stripe Blog — https://stripe.com/blog/stripe-projects-adds-new-agents-providers-developer-controls

### 4) ChatGPT Work fragments conversations across cloud and desktop
- Track: remembering
- Why it matters to me: OpenAI's attempt to clarify ChatGPT Work reveals a design decision with compounding consequences: cloud Work conversations don't appear in desktop Work; desktop threads and local files stay on that computer. This is intentional isolation for security, but it creates a new kind of knowledge fragmentation — exactly the opposite of what an "agent" is supposed to deliver.
- Tradeoff / tension: Security and isolation (keep local files local, keep desktop threads separate) vs. seamless continuity (one conversation, one memory, everywhere). OpenAI chose isolation, which means the user experience of "my AI knows everything about me" is structurally limited by design. Every agentic tool will face this same choice.
- Implication: Fragmentation compounds. Users who start building context in desktop won't see their cloud work and vice versa. This creates lock-in through data silos rather than through features. For the remembering track: this is a cautionary example of why external memory systems need to be unified across contexts, or they become fragmented knowledge traps. If you're building any tool that accumulates knowledge over time, the data flow boundaries are the first thing to design.
- Action: When evaluating or building agentic tools, map the data flow boundaries upfront. Ask: "Where does context get lost?" The answer tells you whether the tool will compound knowledge or fragment it.
- Confidence: medium
- Source: Simon Willison quoting OpenAI — https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jul/10/openai/
