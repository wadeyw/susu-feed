The 28.7% pass rate means AI can assist with hypothesis triage and workflow exploration (as a geneticist confirmed), but it cannot independently produce reliable research. The gap between "can help" and "can replace" is the judgment layer — and that's the hardest layer to automate.
- Implication: This maps directly to the broader question of what AI can and cannot do in knowledge work. The pattern holds across domains: execution gets cheap and reliable first; judgment under ambiguity lags. For daughters growing up in an AI-native world, this means the premium on human judgment, taste, and the ability to know when results are "decision-ready" will increase, not decrease. The skills that matter most are the ones AI is worst at: framing the right problem, recognizing messy-data red flags, and deciding when to stop iterating.
- Action: Think: when evaluating AI output for your own work, develop a checklist for "closing the inferential loop" — does the analysis handle its own edge cases, validate against known constraints, and flag uncertainty? This is the judgment layer you'll need to master whether or not AI improves.
