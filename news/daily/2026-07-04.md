### 2) AI is cannibalizing paid education — and the double-whammy is structural
- Track: ai_change / daughters_future
- Why it matters to me: Josh W. Comeau reports his third course is selling at ~1/3 of typical volume. Multiple course creators confirm 50%+ revenue drops. His diagnosis: a double-whammy where (1) developers are anxious about whether their jobs will exist, so they won't invest in learning new skills, and (2) LLMs now provide personalized tutoring, reducing the incentive to buy structured courses. This isn't just about web dev education — it's a pattern that applies to any skill-based content business. For the daughters' future: if LLMs can tutor effectively, the value proposition of formal instruction shifts dramatically toward what humans do better — judgment, taste, confidence, and the ability to synthesize across domains.
- Tradeoff / tension: LLM tutoring is cheaper and more accessible but lacks the accountability, community, and credentialing that courses provide. The creators who survive will be those who offer outcomes, not information — coaching, portfolio review, and peer networks that an LLM can't replicate. Meanwhile, the anxiety about job viability is itself a self-reinforcing loop: if everyone thinks their skills are obsolete, nobody invests in building new ones, and the skills vacuum accelerates the very disruption they fear.
- Implication: Any business built on teaching or certifying skills needs to rethink its value proposition. Information delivery is commoditized. The premium shifts to human judgment, accountability, and taste development. For daughters' preparation: skills that compound with human context (writing with voice, speaking with presence, designing with taste) become more valuable, not less.
- Action: Evaluate any education or content business through the lens: "Can an LLM do the information delivery part for free?" If yes, the real value must be in outcomes, community, or judgment development.
- Confidence: high

### 3) Let AI agents use judgment instead of micromanagement
- Track: better_decisions / ai_change
- Why it matters to me: Simon Willison's experiment with Claude Code/Fable reveals a pattern that scales beyond coding: give AI agents their own judgment about task complexity and resource allocation, and they optimize better than rigid rules. The specific move — telling Fable to decide which lower-power model to delegate each task to — resulted in "a ton of work" completed while conserving high-tier token allowances. The generated memory file codifies a split: main loop (design, auditing, synthesis) stays on expensive models; subagents (implementation, mechanical edits) use cheaper ones. This is a concrete operating model for human-AI teams.
- Tradeoff / tension: Micromanagement guarantees consistency but burns tokens and slows iteration. Delegation of judgment requires trust in the agent's calibration — which means you need to validate its decisions periodically rather than blocking every step. The sweet spot is "set the goal, let the agent decide the path, audit the outcome." But auditing itself takes time, and over-auditing negates the efficiency gains.
- Implication: This pattern generalizes to any domain where AI assists with layered work — writing, analysis, design, research. The principle: assign judgment to the system closest to the work, reserve human oversight for outcomes and exceptions. For business building: this is how you scale AI-assisted operations without turning agents into expensive yes-machines.
- Action: Apply the "judgment delegation" pattern to your own AI workflows. Identify which tasks can be split into main-loop (judgment) vs subagent (execution) and test token/quality tradeoffs.
- Confidence: medium
- Source: Simon Willison — https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jul/3/judgement/

### 4) Wikipedia's sourcing crisis and the death of engagement farming
- Track: remembering / better_decisions
- Why it matters to me: An analysis of the Odin programming language Wikipedia deletion reveals a deeper structural problem: Wikipedia's requirement for "reliable independent sources" clashes with how software knowledge actually lives — in Discord channels, GitHub repos, personal blogs, and oral community knowledge. The author dissects how both sides of the controversy (GingerBill attacking Wikipedia as "ideological," Jimmy Wales defending procedure) are partly performing for engagement. This is a case study in how information authority is shifting: the gatekeepers (Wikipedia) rely on formal citations, but the actual innovation and expertise live in informal, unindexed networks. For the remembering track: if knowledge is moving from indexed, citable sources to fluid, informal channels, external memory systems need to adapt.
- Tradeoff / tension: Formal sourcing creates verifiability but excludes living knowledge. Informal knowledge is rich and current but ephemeral and uncreditable. Engagement farming exploits both tensions — outrage drives clicks regardless of substance. The meta-insight: the people most likely to frame information disputes as ideological battles are often the ones most invested in the attention economy.
- Implication: Building a personal knowledge system in 2026 means capturing from informal sources (notes, screenshots, transcripts) and structuring them before they vanish. Wikipedia-style curation is becoming a luxury good — useful for established topics, useless for emerging ones. For decision quality: learn to distinguish between genuine sourcing disputes and engagement-farmed outrage. The pattern is consistent — whoever frames a procedural disagreement as an ideological battle is usually amplifying, not resolving.
- Action: Start capturing informal knowledge sources (Discord threads, GitHub discussions, blog comments) in your personal knowledge system before they disappear. Develop a heuristic for spotting engagement farming: look for performers who claim indifference but escalate when challenged.
- Confidence: medium
