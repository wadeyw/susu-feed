### 4) Internet Archive: 15% of dead web pages rescued, but 1 in 4 URLs vanish forever
- Track: remembering
- Why it matters to me: This quantifies the fragility of digital knowledge at scale. 25% of pages from 2013–2023 are now dead. The Wayback Machine rescues ~15% of those — reducing total loss from 1 in 4 to 1 in 10. But 1 in 10 URLs still vanishes completely. This is a concrete data point on why external memory systems matter: the web is not persistent by default.
- Tradeoff / tension: The IA is improving discovery (IndexNow, MediaCloud feeds, Wikipedia EventStream), but the fundamental challenge is resource-constrained crawling vs. the infinite surface of the web. There's also a selection bias: highly-linked content (NY Times outlinks) gets rescued at 96% rates, while obscure but valuable content disappears. The tradeoff is between breadth of coverage and depth of preservation.
- Implication: For anyone building a knowledge system, this reinforces the principle: don't rely on external links as permanent references. Archive what matters locally. The IA's "rescued" category (dead on live web, alive in archive) is exactly the kind of content you want mirrored in your own knowledge base. The pattern is clear — valuable content dies faster than you think, and the ones that survive are the ones that are already well-linked.
- Action: When building the knowledge base, prioritize archiving content that is high-signal but low-link-count (deep technical posts, niche research, personal notes). These are the most vulnerable to vanishing and the hardest to recover later.
- Confidence: medium
- Source: Internet Archive Blog — https://blog.archive.org/2026/04/23/gone-but-not-forgotten-recovering-the-dead-web/
