### 3) A scorecard for the AI age — measuring useful work, not tokens

- **Track:** own_business
- **Why it matters to me:** OpenAI's CFO Sarah Friar published a framework for evaluating AI ROI that shifts the metric from cost-per-token to cost-per-accepted-outcome. While this is fundamentally a sales piece dressed as analysis, the underlying framework is operationally useful: define what "done" means for your team, track tasks completed vs. total cost, measure dependability (ready-to-use vs. needs-correction vs. needs-escalation), and evaluate whether each AI dollar buys more work as usage grows.
- **Tradeoff / tension:** The cheapest model per token is rarely the cheapest per successful outcome. Lower-tier models fail more often, require retries, and demand heavier human correction — all hidden costs that don't show up in API pricing dashboards. The tradeoff is between upfront cost visibility (token pricing is transparent) and true cost opacity (outcome quality depends on task complexity, model capability, and human review overhead). OpenAI's own data shows GPT-5.6 Sol uses 54% fewer output tokens than competitors while achieving higher success rates on coding tasks — but that's their data. Independent verification is scarce.
- **Implication:** If you're evaluating AI tools for your own business or advising others, stop comparing models by token price. Start tracking: acceptance rate, retry frequency, human review time, and total workflow completion cost. These are the metrics that determine whether an AI integration is actually profitable.
- **Action:** When evaluating any AI tool purchase, build a simple spreadsheet: model cost + retry cost + human review time = total cost per outcome. Compare across tiers. The result will often surprise you — the "premium" model may be cheaper per successful task.
- **Confidence:** medium (source bias noted)
