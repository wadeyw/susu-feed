### 4) AI enthusiasts are in a race against time, AI skeptics are in a race against entropy
- Track: better_decisions
- Why it matters to me: This reframes the entire AI adoption debate from "is AI good or bad?" to a structural tension between two different kinds of decay. Enthusiasts race against time because capability jumps keep getting faster — hesitation has a real cost. Skeptics race against entropy because technical debt, team friction, and organizational noise accumulate faster when AI tools are adopted without discipline. Both are right. Both matter.
- Tradeoff / tension: Moving fast with AI creates real capability gains but also real structural problems: prompt injection risks, vendor lock-in, degraded code quality if you don't enforce standards, team confusion about role boundaries. Moving slowly preserves quality and stability but risks being structurally outpaced by competitors or collaborators who lean in. The real tension isn't adopt-vs-resist; it's adopt-with-discipline-vs-adopt-without-discipline.
- Implication: The decision framework should shift from "should we use AI?" to "how do we use AI without creating unmanageable debt?" This means building guardrails (code review standards, evaluation pipelines, security review) at the same pace as adoption. Charity Majors' observation that "the enthusiasts are not wrong" is important, but the follow-up — that skeptics are also not wrong — is the part that actually guides better decisions.
- Action: Evaluate your own projects through the dual-lens: what capability are we gaining from AI adoption, and what structural debt is it creating? Build the debtfighting mechanisms (tests, reviews, guardrails) in parallel with adoption, not after.
- Confidence: high
- Source: Simon Willison — https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/4/ai-enthusiasts-ai-skeptics/#atom-everything
