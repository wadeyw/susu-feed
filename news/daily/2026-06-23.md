### 2) Nearly half of LG smart TV apps silently route traffic through residential proxy networks
- Track: ai_change
- Why it matters to me: This is a concrete example of how trust failures compound in IoT ecosystems. 2,058 of 6,038 apps on LG/Samsung TVs contain residential proxy SDKs — apps people install thinking they're clocks or screensavers are actually monetizing the user's home IP. The risk isn't just IP sharing; the TV sits on the home LAN, and some SDKs (Massive, Honeygain) lack the private-range blocklists that Bright Data includes. A compromised SDK could reach routers, NAS, cameras.
- Tradeoff / tension: Users trade a small privacy risk for free utility apps. Developers get revenue without intrusive ads. Platforms (LG, Samsung) face no liability because the apps are store-approved. But the asymmetry is extreme: the harm is diffuse and delayed, the benefit is immediate and visible. Amazon and Roku have banned these SDKs — LG and Samsung haven't. This is a platform governance signal: who decides what "acceptable" device behavior looks like when the device is always-on, always-connected, and inside the home network.
- Implication: As AI lowers the barrier to creating "benign" apps (screensavers, widgets, clocks), the proxy-sdk business model will spread to more device categories. The structural pattern — consent once, run forever, monetize idle resources — is the template for the next wave of ambient data extraction. For personal security, this means smart TVs should be treated as network-adjacent devices with unknown data exfiltration capability, not as furniture.
- Action: Audit connected devices on your home network. If you have a smart TV, check installed apps for proxy SDKs (look for Bright Data, Massive, Honeygain identifiers). Consider putting IoT devices on a separate VLAN.
- Confidence: medium
- Source: Spur Intelligence Labs — https://spur.us/blog/smart-tv-apps-residential-proxy-sdks

### 4) sqlite-utils 4.0rc1: migrations and nested transactions arrive
- Track: remembering
- Why it matters to me: For anyone building knowledge systems on SQLite (which is basically everyone doing local-first data work), migrations and nested transactions close two critical gaps. Migrations enable schema evolution without destructive resets. Nested transactions allow complex multi-step operations to roll back cleanly without cascading failures. This is infrastructure-level work that makes external memory systems more reliable and less painful to evolve.
- Tradeoff / tension: Adding migrations introduces complexity — you now need to version-control schema changes and handle upgrade paths. Nested transactions add overhead and potential locking contention. But the alternative is fragile systems that break when you need to change course. The tradeoff is reliability vs. simplicity, and for any system you plan to keep growing, reliability wins.
- Implication: The tooling around local-first knowledge bases is maturing. sqlite-utils is a foundational tool in the Datasette/simonw ecosystem, and these features make it viable for more serious knowledge-management workloads. For the broader remembering track: reliable schema evolution means the knowledge base can grow organically without requiring periodic destructive rebuilds.
- Action: If you're using sqlite-utils for any persistent data work, test the rc1 with your current schema. Migrations will likely become essential once you need to add fields to existing tables without losing data.
- Confidence: high
- Source: Simon Willison — https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jun/21/sqlite-utils-40rc1/
