### 2) Agent Traffic Is Now the Majority at Stripe — Infrastructure Is Catching Up
- Track: ai_change
- Why it matters to me: Agent traffic now comprises ~40% of Stripe's documentation traffic and 70% of their CLI API requests come from agents. Stripe is responding by building Stripe Projects as a skill for AI agents — letting agents provision infrastructure, manage credentials, and connect services directly. This is the moment when AI agents stop being code-writing assistants and become infrastructure operators. The infrastructure layer for agent-built software is being standardized right now, and Stripe is defining it. For anyone building software, this means the path from idea to live product is shifting from human-led to agent-mediated.
- Tradeoff / tension: Agent-driven development dramatically compresses the time from idea to deployment, but it also compresses the feedback loop that prevents expensive mistakes. Stripe's new controls (per-provider spend limits, named environments, scoped credentials) are an admission that agents will overspend, misconfigure, or create technical debt at scale. The tradeoff is speed vs. guardrails — and the guardrails are still being written.
- Implication: If you're planning to build software in the next 12-24 months, the default path will likely involve agents provisioning and operating infrastructure. Understanding the emerging control layers (spend limits, environment isolation, credential scoping) is now part of product strategy, not just security.
- Action: watch
- Confidence: high
- Source: Stripe Blog — https://stripe.com/blog/stripe-projects-adds-new-agents-providers-developer-controls

### 3) OpenAI Academy: Standardizing AI Fluency as Enterprise Deployment
- Track: daughters_future
- Why it matters to me: OpenAI launched three courses — AI Foundations, Applied AI Foundations, and Agents and Workflows — developed with BCG, Accenture, and BBVA. The curriculum follows a progression from individual prompting to structured workflows to agent-directed work. This is significant because it represents the first attempt by a model vendor to standardize AI literacy at enterprise scale. For your daughters, this signals what the baseline AI competency will look like in 2-3 years: not just knowing how to prompt, but designing workflows with inputs, models, tools, checkpoints, and human review. The three-tier structure is itself a framework worth studying.
- Tradeoff / tension: Vendor-standardized learning creates consistency and scales adoption, but it also locks in the vendor's mental model of what AI competence looks like. OpenAI's curriculum will shape how enterprises train their workforce, which means it shapes the skill baseline for an entire generation of workers. The question is whether that baseline serves independent thinking or optimized compliance with a specific toolchain.
- Implication: The skills that will matter most are the ones the curriculum emphasizes but doesn't fully cover: critical evaluation of AI output, designing workflows that survive model changes, and maintaining human judgment where it counts. These are transferable skills that won't be taught by any vendor course.
- Action: act
- Confidence: medium
