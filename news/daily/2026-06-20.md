### 1) MCP's real value may be auth isolation, not agent tooling

- Track: ai_change
- Why it matters to me: Sean Lynch's observation reframes the entire Model Context Protocol debate. We've been arguing about whether MCP is the right abstraction for agent tool discovery — but the durable competitive advantage it creates is isolating credential flows outside the agent's context window, potentially outside the harness entirely. This means MCP's long-term value isn't as a "plugin marketplace" but as an auth gateway for AI tooling. If true, the protocol that wins isn't the one with the most tools attached, but the one that owns the trust boundary between agents and their data sources.
- Tradeoff / tension: Centralizing auth in MCP servers creates a new critical dependency. If MCP becomes the de facto auth gateway for AI tooling, a compromise or policy change at that layer affects every agent downstream. It solves the context-window credential leak problem but concentrates trust in fewer hands.
- Implication: Stop evaluating MCP primarily as a tool-discovery standard. Evaluate it as a trust-layer primitive. The companies that build MCP-compatible auth gateways may capture more durable value than those building MCP-compatible tools.
- Action: Watch for MCP server implementations that position themselves as auth intermediaries rather than tool catalogs. Note which ones isolate credentials from agent context.

### 2) AI compresses the maintenance burden of specialized knowledge — a 4.8% yield on "unsolvable" cases

- Track: daughters_future
- Why it matters to me: The OpenAI / Boston Children's Hospital study in NEJM AI shows something structurally important: AI doesn't replace domain experts in rare-disease diagnosis, but it dramatically reduces the cognitive maintenance cost of staying current. New gene-disease links, variant classifications, and case reports emerge constantly while patient genomes don't change. The bottleneck wasn't knowledge — it was time to cross-reference it. 18 new diagnoses from 376 previously unsolved cases (4.8% yield) is modest in absolute terms but represents work that previously required years of expert effort. For the daughters' future, this is the pattern: the valuable skill isn't memorizing domains, it's knowing how to structure questions so AI can surface connections across fragmented knowledge.
- Tradeoff / tension: AI generates hypotheses; humans still validate. The model never diagnosed — it needed ACMG/AMP framework review, two-team consensus, and CLIA-certified lab confirmation. The yield comes from the human-AI loop, not the AI alone. This means the future belongs to people who can effectively participate in that loop: asking good questions, reviewing outputs critically, and knowing when to trust vs. verify.
- Implication: Teaching children to work in diagnostic loops with AI — not to replace expertise, but to amplify its reach — is more valuable than teaching rote domain knowledge. The ability to structure problems for AI review and interpret AI-generated leads is the new literacy.
- Action: Consider how writing and structured reasoning practice (which builds question-formulation ability) compounds into AI-augmented expertise. This favors daughters who learn to think clearly about problems, not just memorize answers.
- Confidence: high
- Source: OpenAI News — https://openai.com/index/diagnose-rare-childhood-diseases
