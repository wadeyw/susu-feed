### 2) AI advice doesn't just make people wrong — it erodes the habit of knowing what they don't know
- Track: daughters_future + better_decisions
- Why it matters to me: A new study found that access to AI advice dropped accuracy by 3x and willingness to say "I don't know" by 83%, while confidence doubled. This isn't just "people overtrust AI." It's that AI availability actively suppresses metacognition — the ability to recognize the boundaries of your own knowledge. For your daughters, this is a core skill to protect: the capacity to say "I don't know" is foundational to learning, and AI is training a generation to skip that step.
- Tradeoff / tension: AI is genuinely useful as a verification aid or starting point. But the design pattern of AI systems — answer confidently, never hedge — gets internalized. Monetary incentives only marginally improved honesty (3% → 8% willingness to admit ignorance), suggesting this is deep psychological dependency, not just lazy behavior.
- Implication: Teach intellectual humility as a counterweight to AI convenience. The goal isn't to avoid AI, but to build the habit of: (1) forming your own answer first, (2) checking against AI, (3) noticing when AI makes you *more confident but less accurate*. That third step is the metacognitive skill that protects against cognitive surrender.
- Action: **Act.** When your daughters use AI for learning, ask them to write their answer first, then compare. Practice saying "I was wrong about X" without it feeling like failure. The skill is recovering accuracy, not avoiding mistakes.
- Confidence: high
- Source: TNW reporting on Capraro et al. study — https://thenextwin.com/news/ai-advice-suppresses-critical-thinking-wrong-answers-study
