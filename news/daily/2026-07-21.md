### 1) Reverse-engineering is cheap now
- Track: ai_change
- Why it matters to me: The cost floor for useful software is dropping in a non-obvious way. Simon points to a pattern he's been hearing repeatedly: people using coding agents to reverse-engineer and automate home devices. Before agents, the work was possible but the ROI was negative — undocumented APIs, unstable interfaces, one-off problems. Now the marginal cost of writing the glue code is near zero, which means a whole class of projects that were never worth anyone's time suddenly become viable. This is the same structural shift as "programming is becoming a general skill" but at the application layer: not just building new things, but unlocking old ones.
- Tradeoff / tension: Simon flags the real risk himself — undocumented, unstable APIs may change or break. Cheap reverse-engineering creates fragile automation that works today and fails tomorrow. The question is whether the value of unlocking the device outweighs the maintenance burden of fighting its hidden interface.
- Implication: The next wave of useful software won't come from well-documented platforms. It'll come from people probing the edges of things that were previously too expensive to understand. This favors builders who can navigate ambiguity and read systems from the outside in.
- Action: Think — what in your life or work is currently locked behind an undocumented interface, awkward workflow, or "too much effort to automate"? That's now a viable project.

### 4) Who's Afraid of Chinese Models?
- Track: better_decisions
- Why it matters to me: Ben Thompson's proposal — the US should legally protect data collection for training as fair use and bar ToS clauses that forbid distillation — is a concrete policy lever with real consequences. It addresses a hypocrisy: labs outlaw distillation while training on unlicensed data. More importantly, it frames the competitive dynamic with Chinese models not as a technology race but as a legal-institutional one. If the US doesn't act, Chinese open models may pull ahead not because they're smarter but because the legal environment lets them iterate faster.
- Tradeoff / tension: Protecting data collection as fair use enables innovation but raises copyright concerns. Banning ToS anti-distillation clauses helps US labs compete but weakens contractual freedom. The tension is between national competitiveness and individual/platform rights. There's no clean solution — only prioritization.
- Implication: The AI race is being shaped by law as much as by compute. Builders should pay attention to policy developments in IP, data rights, and distillation, because they will determine which ecosystems produce the most capable models over the next 2-3 years.
- Action: Watch — track this proposal. If it gains traction, it changes the competitive landscape for open models in a way that affects product strategy.
