### 2) Writing Is Thinking — Not Expression. Using AI to Write Is Abdication.
- Track: daughters_future
- Why it matters to me: This is about the daughters' future-readiness in a critical skill: the ability to think clearly and communicate precisely. Marco Borretti's piece argues that writing isn't a secondary step after thinking — it's the thinking process itself. When you use AI to turn bullet points into prose, you're not delegating implementation; you're outsourcing the act of making vague ideas concrete, which is where real understanding happens. The Josef Weizenbaum quote is essential: "Our pen writes the word 'because' and suddenly stops. We thought we understood the 'why' of something, but discover that we don't."
- Tradeoff / tension: AI writing tools feel efficient in the short term (produce polished text from rough notes instantly) but degrade the writer's ability to think clearly over time. The competence inference is sharp: if you can't convert basic bullet points into coherent prose, your underlying ideas are likely incoherent. There's no unfalsifiable shield — poor writing exposes poor thinking, and AI-generated writing hides that exposure from the writer while forcing the reader to do the logic verification.
- Implication: For daughters' development, insist on writing as a discipline, not just an output format. The ability to sit with a vague idea and force it into precise language is a compounding skill that AI can't replicate on their behalf. In an AI-saturated world, the ability to distinguish your own thinking from machine-generated coherence becomes a competitive advantage.
- Action: think — when evaluating tools for daughters' education, prioritize those that strengthen writing-as-thinking rather than those that produce writing without thinking.
- Confidence: medium
