### 2) Datasette Apps: External memory systems get interactive frontends

- Track: remembering
- Why it matters to me: Datasette now supports embedding self-contained HTML+JS applications inside its iframe sandbox, with read-write SQL access via stored queries. This transforms Datasette from a read-only data viewer into a platform for building custom knowledge interfaces — dashboards, tools, forms, visualizations — all backed by your relational data. Crucially, it was built with AI-assisted development (Codex Desktop, GPT-5.5 xhigh) and security audited by Claude Fable 5, closing a privilege-escalation vulnerability during development.
- Tradeoff / tension: The iframe sandbox is tight — no localStorage, no cookie access, CSP blocks all outbound HTTP by default. This is secure but constraining. Writing requires Stored Queries to be explicitly enabled. The tradeoff is security vs. flexibility: you get a safe sandbox but lose direct control over the runtime environment. The `apps-set-csp` permission gives admins the ability to allow-list external CDNs, but regular users are locked to pre-approved origins.
- Implication: This is a concrete step toward making your external knowledge base interactive and tool-like rather than just queryable. If you've been treating Datasette as a passive archive, this opens the door to building actual applications on top of it — personal dashboards, project trackers, decision logs with rich UIs. The AI-assisted development story (Claude Fable 5 finding the security hole, Codex building the prototype) also demonstrates a new pattern: AI writing and auditing the infrastructure that holds your external memory.
- Action: Experiment. Build one small app inside Datasette to test the pattern — perhaps a personal project tracker or a decision journal with filters. The stored query + iframe sandbox is the primitive; what you build with it is the value.
- Confidence: medium

### 3) LifeSciBench: AI's real bottleneck isn't reasoning — it's artifact interpretation

- Track: better_decisions
- Why it matters to me: OpenAI's new LifeSciBench evaluates AI on 750 expert-authored life science research tasks with 19,020 rubric criteria across 7 workflows. The key finding: frontier models drop from 45.1% pass rate on text-only tasks to 28.1% when artifacts (figures, PDFs, tables, sequences) are involved. Numeric exact-output tasks sit at 14.8%. Construct generation at 27.3%. Meanwhile, scientific communication hits 71.1% and translation 57.7%. The pattern is clear: AI excels at language about work, struggles with the actual work.
- Tradeoff / tension: The benchmark rewards partial correctness and operational utility — a model can earn >50% rubric credit while still failing the exact pass threshold in ~14% of tasks. This means "mostly right" is common and dangerous in high-stakes domains. The granular rubrics (25 criteria per task, avg.) expose where models are confident but wrong, which is worse than models that admit uncertainty.
- Implication: For decision-making, this quantifies a fundamental limitation: AI is a reasoning amplifier over text, not a perception engine over complex evidence. If your work involves interpreting figures, sequences, or multi-modal artifacts, AI will systematically underperform. This is a filter for where to invest human effort vs. AI augmentation. For daughters' future-readiness, the lesson is concrete: teach them to interpret complex evidence (charts, data, documents) directly — that's the irreplaceable skill.
- Action: Think. Map your own work to this pattern: where do you rely on AI for artifact-heavy tasks? Those are the places to double down on human judgment or improve the AI's artifact pipeline.
- Confidence: medium
- Source: OpenAI News — https://openai.com/index/introducing-life-sci-bench
