## What materially matters today

### 1) Understand to participate — the cognitive debt trap of coding agents

- Track: better_decisions
- Why it matters to me: Geoffrey Litt's framing at AIE hits the core tension of the next phase of AI collaboration. As coding agents construct increasingly large and sophisticated changes, your understanding naturally drifts from how the code actually works. That drift is cognitive debt — and it's compounding interest. You can delegate execution but you cannot delegate judgment without paying the understanding tax first. This applies equally to evaluating AI tools for your daughters' education (can they judge what the agent produces?) and to your own business (can you audit agent-built systems?).
- Tradeoff / tension: Deep understanding slows you down today but prevents catastrophic misalignment tomorrow. Shallow participation lets you move fast now but creates a knowledge black box that becomes impossible to debug when things break. The cost of understanding is measured in hours today; the cost of not understanding is measured in irrecoverable trust loss later.
- Implication: The skill that compounds in an agent-heavy world isn't prompt engineering — it's maintaining sufficient fluency in the underlying system to participate meaningfully. This is a meta-skill: learning how to learn the internals of systems you're delegating to. For your daughters, this means teaching them to ask "how does this actually work?" not just "what does this do?" For your business, it means allocating time for code archaeology when agents write more code than you review.
- Action: Build a weekly "understanding checkpoint" habit: pick one system you're currently delegating to an agent, and spend 30 minutes tracing through its logic without assistance. Document what you find. This is cheaper than debugging a failed deployment at 2 AM.

### 2) DSPy as a methodology for improving AI system prompts

- Track: remembering
- Why it matters to me: Simon used Claude Fable 5 to autonomously set up a DSPy pipeline that evaluated and improved Datasette Agent's SQL system prompts. The insight isn't the tool — it's the pattern: using automated evaluation (gold-standard datasets, custom metrics, live tool invocation) to iteratively improve prompts rather than hand-tuning them. This turns prompt engineering from an art into a measurable discipline. For your knowledge base, this demonstrates a reusable workflow: collect examples → define metrics → let the system optimize → validate. For daughters' future skills, it models computational thinking applied to language — treating prompts as code that can be tested and improved.
- Tradeoff / tension: DSPy-style evaluation adds infrastructure overhead. You need a test harness, a gold-standard dataset, and metric definitions before you see benefits. For small projects, hand-tuning may be faster. But once you cross a threshold of complexity (multi-step agents, production systems), the evaluation framework pays for itself. The tension is knowing when to invest in the machinery versus when to just tweak the prompt.
- Implication: The frontier of AI application building is shifting from "writing better prompts" to "building better evaluation pipelines." Whoever masters the evaluation layer controls the quality layer. This is infrastructure work that compounds — a good eval harness for one domain can be adapted to others.
- Action: Identify one prompt-heavy workflow in your current projects. Define 3-5 concrete success criteria for it. Test 3 variations against those criteria. Don't need DSPy — a simple spreadsheet with scored outputs works. Start measuring before you automate.
- Confidence: medium
- Source: Simon Willison — https://simonwillison.net/2026/Jul/2/dspy-datasette-agent-prompts/

## Idea sparks

- The "understand to participate" principle generalizes beyond coding: it applies to financial systems, legal processes, medical decisions, and any domain where you're increasingly delegating to AI. Build a checklist: "Do I understand this well enough to catch a serious error?" If the answer is no, don't delegate yet.
- DSPy-style evaluation as a personal learning tool: instead of just consuming AI outputs, build a scoring rubric for what "good" looks like in your domain. Over time, this rubric becomes your externalized judgment — a form of remembering that doesn't rely on memory.

## Life / family nudge

- When talking with your daughters about AI, resist the urge to focus on tools. Focus on the deeper question: "Can you explain how this works to someone who doesn't use it?" That ability to translate — to understand deeply enough to teach — is what separates participants from consumers. Practice it together: pick something she learned this week and ask her to explain it to you without using the jargon she just learned.
